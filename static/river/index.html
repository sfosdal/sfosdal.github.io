Our Story

For as long as most of the neighborhood can remember, we've been Lower Queen Anne's living room — dark, warm, gloriously cluttered, and open to absolutely everybody.

We've been pouring since the mid-1950s, and spent decades on West Mercer Street — right up until the end of 2014, when the block got bought out from under us for the new South Korean Consulate (a cool $2.4 million). Most bars would've just called it. Instead we packed up the whole place — the wall paneling, the tile, even the toilets — and hauled it a few blocks to the old Jabu's Pub at 174 Roy St. We reopened on Friday the 13th of February 2015, and we've held this corner ever since.

You can thank Mike Lewis and Mary McIntyre for keeping the lights on. Mike, a former Seattle Post-Intelligencer columnist, put his severance into the bar when the P-I printed its last edition back in 2009. A stone's throw from Seattle Center and Climate Pledge Arena, we've stayed exactly what we've always been: a low-lit room with a wraparound bar, walls layered in chalk, old photos, band flyers and years of regulars' handwriting, cold beer, and honest pours.

The neighborhood's changed plenty — condos, cranes, a shiny arena down the street — but we haven't, much. This is still the rare corner where a first-timer and a longtime regular wind up elbow to elbow and nobody blinks: pre-show crowds, off-shift cooks, dog-walkers, and folks who just live around the block, all coming through the same door. The local press has had a soft spot for us, too — Eater, Seattle Weekly, and Axios have all written up the Streamline over the years — and we're still a regular on the neighborhood's nights-out lists.

We're quirky, a little punk, and we feel like home. Everyone's welcome here, exactly as you are — every name, every pronoun. And we're seriously dog-friendly: well-behaved pups are regulars too, with a water bowl by the door, a scratch behind the ears, and the run of the place right beside you.

Come in for one and stay for four, or nurse a soda in the corner and just take it all in — both are completely fine by us. We run on one simple idea: a neighborhood needs a room like this, and everyone deserves a seat in it.
